The Witness

 

Three days, it seems, since we entered the Tejproc Sea.  At first glance, the waves are barren.  A wide, wrinkled blanket of water laid over the restless sleep of the world.  It seems, out here upon the waves, that the whole planet is as ill at ease as I am.  Far from the light and hope of shore, there is only the barest memory of warmth or comfort left after our trials.  Because the sea is not dead, not barren.  From her watery womb are born the unimaginable.  We have seen, we have witnessed.  

          

Before we entered the Tejproc, in those far off happy times, we had been at sea near on two months.  I myself had lost track of the days, but Captain Miller kept regular intervals with his timepiece.  This is my first voyage but the rest of the crew seem old hands.  There were but four of us on the small ship.   A skeleton crew, we were called, though the term now makes me shiver.  There was Captain Miller, a tough but knowledgeable man, Mr. Bond, a jovial fellow who delighted in laughter and any manner of amusement, and Mr. Gerrits, a constant and dedicated man, whose resolve stood stronger than most in such adversity.  Messers Bond and Gerrits manned the ship well, clambering through masts and rigging with an agility and skill that left me awed.  Mr. Bond was also our signalman, and never was I more assured a message would go through than when it was in his hands.  I was on as a passenger and temporary surgeon.  Captain Miller, of course, had command of us all.  I did not think I could ever doubt them. But now we have seen, now we have witnessed.

 

The first days were warm and happy.  We made good time and the winds were favorable.  There was a laughter and joy on the deck and decent food and conversation in the evenings.  Though I regarded the crossing of the Tejproc with some anticipation, Captain Miller kept my spirits high with tales of his many successful and adventurous voyages.  One morning, as I stood on the deck, I spotted grey fins, tall and curved breaking the water.  I called to the men, my voice filled with concern, fearing the appearance of a group of sharks, but Mr. Gerrits informed me, choking back his laughter, that they were dolphins, and a sign of good fortune.  The beasts came closer and I watched with awe their graceful movements.  I could not imagine such beauty before, and I trembled with joy at the notion of a sea so full of incredible creatures.  But that was before we had seen, before we had witnessed.

 When we began our crossing of the Tejproc, immediately there was a change.  Captain Miller became more intense, more driven and less permissive of the laxity that we had grown accustomed to.  Mr. Gerrits and Mr. Bond changed as well, growing more inward, more focused.  I suppose I changed my attitude as well, growing more attentive to my task and supporting the crew in any way I could.  Before long, as always seems the way in the Tejproc, the skies grew foul.  The wind, it seemed, came from every direction contrary to what we needed, and the clouds grew an ominous black and purple, as though the heavens had taken some terrible blow in this spot and now hung bruised above the earth.  I grew increasingly uneasy, and Captain Miller, now consumed with his task, had little time to comfort me.  A pall grew over the whole ship, and the first night was long and terrible.  I never had imagined I could hear worse sounds than those we heard that night upon the deck.  Before we had seen, before we had witnessed.

 

Yesterday, I think, time has a funny way of sliding here, Mr. Bond spotted an Island.  Captain Miller consulted his charts and found that it was uncharted.  He made a note and, seeing as it was growing late, moored us off the coast.  I was dispatched to reconnoiter the island, in truth little more than a rock, while the rest of the crew made repairs.  After rowing the dinghy out to the shore, I walked across the tiny spit of land, never imagining the possibilities that lay beneath because I still had not seen, I still had not witnessed.  

 

    The island was rocky and unforgiving.  No evidence of vegetation or animals, no trace that life had ever touched the stony surface remained on the desolate outcrop.  I walked across it, no more than 50 paces in width and 125 in length. At the extremes the island was unimpressive, a pebble in the immense pond of the ocean.  The only remarkable feature at all was a small arc of stones near the center of the island.  With an hour of daylight or so left, I elected to investigate it before heading back to the miserable vessel floating nearby.  To my pleasure and surprise, upon investigation the stones proved to be carved with elaborate indescribable symbols.  How to describe those symbols, those long winding strings of script that seemed to flow one into the next? They were at once astonishing and repulsive, rapturous and horrid.  The arc that I had seen was not an arc, but instead the borders of a well, half broken away into a semi circle.  The well was deep and as I approached it my flesh began to crawl.  My thoughts were filled with a desire to flee, to escape, to run back to the creaking ship and the lately humorless crew, but I pressed on, compelled by the desire to look, the desire to see what could possibly cause such a chill to run down my spine.  I walked up to the well, to the wall of symbols and looked down.  There I saw.  There I witnessed.

 

    Ah the well! I still can see it whenever I close my eyes, its terrible wonder is seared onto my very spirit.  When Heaven takes me, if they take me now, all across the body of my soul shall be written those symbols.  They ran and spiraled together all down the sides of that resplendent, sickening well. As I looked down in wonderment at the circling script, tracing the delicate spirals down the walls, my gaze fell to the bottom of the well.  The bottom, when compared to the inscribed walls was so plain as to seem an offense against their grandeur.  Until it moved.  I watched in breathless horror as the stony bottom slipped sideways to reveal a massive yellow eye. Watery and jaundiced, the eye rolled down to focus.  Once the enormous X shaped pupil locked on to me, my limbs fell lifeless to my sides.  I stared into the beastâ€™s cyclopean eye, helplessly searching, but it stared into me and then I fell away.  I rose beyond myself, transcending the ship, the island, the sea.  I rose beyond the stars and time until I stood before the eye.  It stared at me-nay, into me, and from it poured forth the truth.  A truth written in the symbols from the well.  They rushed upon me, and brought with them understanding, a terrible understanding that stripped and flayed my soul until it stood ragged at the pinnacle of knowledge.  Then I fell.  Down from that lofty perch I plummeted through the well, down from the heights of understanding to the deepest depths of madness.  I saw. I witnessed.  


    I know not how I found myself in my bunk, I tell you honestly.  Out the porthole I see only the grey sea, no trace of the island nor its terrible well, nor the great eye that lay at the bottom.  All I know is that I was awoken by a horrible, indescribable screaming and howling.  I rushed to deck and there I found three creatures.  Mutilated beyond identification, two sat crouched over the third.  In a ragged talon, one held a bloodied knife, freshly dripping from its carving.  All of them turned to glare at me from empty sockets. The whole of their thin, tormented bodies were scarred, the symbols from the well, cut deep into their flesh.  They howled again and rushed towards me, but I dove back in and locked the door to my cabin.  I do not know who will read this but I am sure now that this ship will never reach Hampshire.  The door will not hold long and even if it did I doubt it will make much difference.  Even now I find myself eyeing the nib of my pen, feel the pull to plunge it into my arm, to write in flesh the understanding I have seen.  God help me, and you, if you read this, because then maybe you too will see.  You too will witness.
